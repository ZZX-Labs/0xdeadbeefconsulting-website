Publication Standards

Readable, Verifiable, and Maintainable

Public resources are intended to be technically useful, clearly scoped, reproducible where practical, and explicit about limitations, assumptions, status, and risk.

Source Provenance

Research should identify relevant sources, dependencies, prior work, datasets, versions, and attribution whenever practical.

Version Information

Software and documentation should identify versions, revisions, publication dates, compatibility, and known limitations.

Reproducibility

Commands, configuration, dependencies, examples, and expected results should be specific enough to verify.

Security Awareness

Resources should avoid exposing secrets, credentials, private data, unsafe defaults, or operationally sensitive information.

Explicit Status

Experimental, incomplete, deprecated, private, unsupported, or historical material should be labeled accordingly.

Correction Process

Errors, vulnerabilities, broken instructions, and inaccurate information should be reportable and subject to controlled revision.

No universal warranty: Public resources are provided for research, education, development, and reference. Users remain responsible for testing, validation, authorization, backups, security, and suitability before use in production or consequential environments.
